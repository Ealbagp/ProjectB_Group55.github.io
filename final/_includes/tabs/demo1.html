Air Pollution, Climate, and Health in NYC

In recent years, rising concern over climate change and air pollution has prompted global efforts to reduce harmful emissions. Among the most dangerous pollutants is PM2.5—microscopic particles that can enter the lungs and bloodstream, contributing to a range of health problems.

Current Air

NYC’s PM2.5 levels are now significantly lower than a decade ago.

How We Breathe

Air quality varies across boroughs, shaped by buildings, traffic, and regional sources.

What’s Changed

PM2.5 levels have declined steadily—down over 44% since 2009.

Why This Matters

While today's values are encouraging, it wasn’t always this way. In the early 2010s, PM2.5 concentrations were much higher, exposing New Yorkers to increased health risks. This dashboard explores how pollution levels have changed—and why it matters. Fine particles like PM2.5 are linked to diseases such as asthma, particularly in vulnerable communities. Understanding this connection is crucial for designing healthier urban environments.
