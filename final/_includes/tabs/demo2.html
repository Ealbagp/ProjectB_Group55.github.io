Breathing NYC's Air: How Harmful Is It, Really?

We often hear that air pollution is bad for our health, but it's difficult to grasp just how bad in tangible terms. Let's focus on PM2.5 , one of the most dangerous forms of air pollution. PM2.5 refers to fine particulate matter smaller than 2.5 micrometers, tiny enough to bypass the body's defenses and penetrate deep into the lungs. To make the risk easier to understand, researchers have depeloped and eye-opening analogy:

Breathing NYC air in 2009 was roughly equivalent to smoking ~182 cigarettes per year
 (Source: Air Pollution and Cigarette Equivalence in Berkeley Earth by Richard A. Muller and Elizabeth A. Muller).


Bearing in mind the equivalent number of cigarettes this represents, it is clear that such exposure can lead to lung diseases. In fact, studies reveal that high levels of PM2.5 exposure can exacerbate asthma and may even induce it. Sources: Exposure to PM2.5 and mortality, Impact of PM2.5 on asthma ER visits.

What does the data tell us?

The Burden of Pollution in Cigarette Equivalents

In 2009, New Yorkers breathed air equivalent to smoking 182 cigarettes/year (~0.5 cigarettes/day). By 2022, thanks to improving air quality, this dropped to 101 cigarettes/year. Although that's a 44% reduction , it still represents a chronic exposure known to irritate airways and inflame lungs.

The 2015 Asthma Surge

A striking spike in asthma hospitalizations occurred in 2015, standing out clearly on the plot. When you shift the asthma curve forward by ~4 years, this spike appears to follow a period (2010‚Äì2013) where PM2.5 levels were relatively high (150‚Äì175 cigarettes/year). This suggests a plausible delayed effect: Chronic exposure to higher PM2.5 in the early 2010s may have increased vulnerability, triggering elevated asthma exacerbations by 2015.
